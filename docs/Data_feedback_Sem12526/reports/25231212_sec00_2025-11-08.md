### Key Differences: Can vs. Could

**1. Modality: Expressing Possibility vs. Ability**
- **Can**:  
  - Describes present ability or possibility‚Äîsomething is realistic, likely, or generally true.
  - Example: ‚ÄúIndividuals can influence government decisions‚Ä¶‚Äù (means they are truly able to do this in the present situation.)
- **Could**:  
  - Describes hypothetical, less certain, or potential ability. Sometimes used for polite or tentative suggestions.
  - Example: ‚ÄúIndividuals could affect government decisions‚Ä¶‚Äù (suggests it may be possible, but it‚Äôs less definite‚Äîperhaps more theoretical or only under certain conditions.)

**2. Tone: Confidence vs. Caution**
- **Can** makes your statement sound confident and assertive.  
  - It tells your reader you believe this is a real, present possibility‚Äîyour claim is stronger.
- **Could** gives a softer, more cautious tone.  
  - It might seem less sure‚Äîsuggesting that this isn‚Äôt always possible or is less direct.

**3. Academic Writing: Precision and Strength**
- In academic essays, **can** is usually preferred when you want to make clear, strong claims‚Äîespecially in your thesis or topic sentences.
- **Could** is useful if you want to discuss conditions, limitations, or possibilities that are less certain.

**Linguistic Concepts in Action:**
- **Modals** (can, could, may, might) are verbs that express possibility, ability, permission, or necessity.
  - **Can** is an example of an epistemic modal expressing factual possibility or capability.
  - **Could** is a conditional/hypothetical modal, showing uncertainty or potentiality.
