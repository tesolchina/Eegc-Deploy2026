---

### Basic Difference

- **Can:** Expresses **ability**, **possibility**, or *general truth* in the present or future; it is more **direct** and **strong**.
- **Could:** Often expresses **conditional possibility**, **suggestion**, **politeness**, or refers to *past ability*; it is often more **tentative** or **less certain**.

---

### Linguistic Concepts

1. **Modality** (Modals show the attitude of the writer or speaker):
   - **Can** is a modal verb indicating *ability* or *factual possibility.*
     - Example: ‚ÄúPeople can influence the government.‚Äù *(It is possible/able for them to do this.)*
   - **Could** is a modal verb indicating *possibility under certain conditions* or *less certainty*.
     - Example: ‚ÄúPeople could influence the government, if they‚Ä¶‚Äù *(It‚Äôs possible, but less definite; maybe only in some cases.)*

2. **Degree of Certainty**
   - **Can** = High certainty (**assertive/strong**): You believe this is really possible or true.
   - **Could** = Lower certainty (**tentative/weak**): You think it might happen, but you‚Äôre not sure.

3. **Formality/Tentativeness**
   - **Could** sometimes makes a claim softer, less direct, or more polite (which is useful for suggestions, but less effective if you want to show a *strong, academic argument*).

---

### Example in Academic Writing

- **Stronger, more academic claim:**  
  *‚ÄúIndividuals can influence government policy by voting and protesting.‚Äù*  
  *(Direct, confident‚Äîmatches a strong thesis!)*
  
- **Weaker or more hypothetical claim:**  
  *‚ÄúIndividuals could influence government policy by voting and protesting.‚Äù*  
  *(Suggests it might be possible, but you are not entirely sure, or perhaps that it depends on other things.)*

---

### When to Use Each?

- Use **can** when you want to **assert** that something is *possible and real*‚Äîgood for main arguments and topic sentences (especially when your thesis is confident).
- Use **could** when you want to be **cautious**, or when something *might happen only under certain conditions*.
